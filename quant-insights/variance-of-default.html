It’s not default — it’s the variance of default

7 mincredit quant

It is not default itself, but the variance of default, that is the real risk to a business. If default is stable you can argue it can be priced in; if the variance is high, it cannot — and that is the part most lenders underestimate.

Level of default

Ironically, given the shape of the default distribution, the level of default and the variance of default are correlated: the higher the default, the higher its variance. The levers that control default are the same levers that control its variance.

Think of a marginal customer — a microfinance borrower for whom ₹5,000 makes the difference. One small event wipes them out this month; they come back the next. Mathematically this is a Bernoulli variable: if p is the annual chance of default, the variance of a unit default is p(1-p). The insight is that not everything can be priced in, and not all levels of default are acceptable.

Tenor

The easiest way to reduce default has been to lengthen tenor: a longer tenor lowers the EMI burden, so per-period default falls. But while default per period falls, the variance of default across the life of the loan rises — if p is the per-period default, the variance is n × p(1-p) and total life default is n × p.

Picture a 6-inch ruler clamped at one end versus a 12-inch one: for the same impulse, the longer ruler swings with a higher amplitude. This is exactly the role of duration in bond pricing — higher duration means higher convexity and more price volatility for a given move in rates or spread. Add the slower run-off of principal and the default variance is higher still. The hedge: FOIRs and LTVs must be lower for longer tenors, not the same.

FOIR and EMI-to-ABB

The usual thinking is that FOIR should be adjusted only for the credit score. Consider customer A — 750+ CIBIL, income ₹5,000 — versus customer B — 750+, income ₹5,00,000. Who should get the higher FOIR? At 90% FOIR, B still has ₹50,000 of disposable income; A has ₹500. FOIR and EMI/ABB must be adjusted for credit quality and size, not score alone.

There is a second, quieter reason. At a low income or bank balance the estimate of income itself carries more variance — being ₹100 off on ₹5,000 (2%) is very different from ₹100 off on ₹50,000 (0.2%). For that reason too, the FOIR or EMI/ABB should be lower at a smaller base.

Loan-to-value

The beautiful part of valuation in asset-backed lending is that the valuation is a myth and the loan is the reality — especially in LAP and used-car lending. If the asset value is small, reduce the LTV, because the error in valuation is larger at a lower base. For a longer tenor the valuation can drift a great deal, so hedge for it; and slower principal run-off means slower equity build-up. Net-net, longer tenors should carry lower LTVs.
