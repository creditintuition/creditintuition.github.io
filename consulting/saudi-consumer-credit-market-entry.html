Saudi Arabia’s credit gap is widest where banks can’t go

16 min readSaudi Arabia · payday + microfinancemarket entry

The brief: is there a venture here — and how would you build it?

An anonymised GCC investor asked a simple question: does Saudi Arabia’s consumer-credit market have room for a new entrant, and if so, what should it look like? This is the resulting market-entry study — market sizing, a competitive read, a strategy, an alternative-data underwriting engine, and five-year unit economics.

Client details are withheld. Figures below are built on public GASTAT and SAMA data plus modelled estimates, and are illustrative of the analytical approach rather than a live business plan.

Even with every current competitor combined, fewer than 10% of the expat payday TAM and under 2% of the expat microfinance TAM is formally served. The question isn’t how to beat incumbents in their segment — it’s how to build the underwriting infrastructure to reach the 12+ million customers nobody has.

The vision: a payday-to-microfinance lender for the underserved.

Build a leading payday and microfinance lender for the geography, focused on the underserved informal-Saudi and expat segments, using alternative data, ML/AI and a fully digital stack — reaching the customers formal lenders structurally skip.

Five pillars, each built on what incumbents cannot do.

01 · where banks cannot follow

Expat & informal segment first

Target the 6.6M expat and 0.72M informal-Saudi TAM, structurally excluded from bank payday (no SADAD salary link, no SIMAH history). The first mover to build alternative underwriting for this segment owns the data moat.

02 · don’t skip the data factory

Payday → microfinance via loan-cycle data

Launch payday to accumulate behavioural data at 6–12 cycles a year. A borrower who repays 4–6 cycles cleanly is a verified good payor — the proprietary signal used to upgrade them into microfinance without relying on SIMAH.

03 · bureau is thin — use everything else

Alternative underwriting / AI-ML stack

Four layers for thin-file customers: telecom & device (top-up frequency, SIM age, device value as income proxy); bank-statement scoring; SIMAH where available, not as a gate; and employer verification.

04 · no branches, no field agents, no paper

Fully digital origination & fulfilment

App onboarding → Iqama/passport OCR + liveness → statement parse → NACH mandate → disbursal in <4 hours. A cost-to-income target of 35–40% versus incumbents’ 60%+ funds aggressive acquisition pricing.

05 · market is nascent, not competitive

Penetration play — volume before margin

Price to penetrate in Years 1–2 (5% flat fee versus informal lenders at 15–20%); extract margin in Year 3+ as behavioural data enables tiered pricing of proven payors. In a nascent market, share of data beats share of margin.

The edge is an alternative-data underwriting engine.

For thin-file borrowers, the model reads four data layers instead of a bureau score:

  • Application — age, employer category, dependents, education, salaried flag, address/pincode.
  • Device & telecom — device make/age/price, data usage, SIM vintage, number porting/recycling, app footprint (financial, loan, gambling), call and SMS patterns.
  • Banking — 3/6-month credit stability and growth, salary regularity, bounce rates, remittance and related-party patterns, regular expenses like rent and school fees.
  • Bureau & cycle — SIMAH where available, DPD strings and tradeline velocity; and, once on book, clean-cycle counts and max-DPD per cycle.

A five-stage flow turns those signals into a decision: (1) dedupe and hard-reject (negative area/profile, fraud, AML); (2) a cycle-bucketed ML credit score (random forest / GBM); (3) line & tenor by DTI and risk band, with expat cut-offs tied to visa duration; (4) pricing; (5) disbursal. Each loan cycle retrains the model on fresh behaviour.

Amount, tenor and price all flex by risk bin and cycle.

Amount caps and tenor widen as a borrower completes clean cycles and their risk bin improves — a Bin-1 borrower can move from SAR 5k to SAR 16k over cycles, while a Bin-5 borrower stays short and small. Price is built as a full waterfall, not a flat rate:

Rate = cost of funds + acquisition cost + default cost + operations cost + target ROA (5% post-tax). The default term is priced on risk grade and the variance of default (μ + σ) — adjusting only for the mean underprices risk. Processing fee and rate are a function of risk grade, amount, tenor and channel cost.

Payday runs on one combined acquisition/behaviour/collections scorecard; microfinance splits them, with separate credit, behavioural and collections models.

The operating stack: originate, manage, collect — agentic and LLM-native.

The build-versus-buy split puts origination and underwriting in-house and procures the loan-management system. Origination is an agentic, LLM-native flow: an LLM/chat LOS extracts information with no forms; an SMS and device scraper plus open-banking and bureau APIs feed it; DS APIs orchestrate underwriting with credit policy executed via RAG; and fulfilment runs KYC/Video-KYC, e-NACH and digital signing. Each step is an AI agent.

Collections is likewise agentic, driven by three variables — amount, DPD and collections risk bucket — escalating from WhatsApp/SMS nudges to pre-EMI and soft/hard calls, then visits for large cases and legal notices. Every step is an agent except the physical visit.
