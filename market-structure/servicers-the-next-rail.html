Servicers — the next rail in lending

8 mintheme 01

I bumped into the need for a servicer indirectly, while studying Pass-Through Certificates (PTCs) in the Indian market.

In my experience, unsecured-loan PTC deals have strong credit protection built into the structure — subordination and cash collateral, typically 10–13%, and on top of that excess interest spread of nearly ⅓ of the rate charged to customers. For MSME PTCs the numbers are around 8% (⅓ of 25%). All in, roughly 20% protection is built in. Stress-testing the structures in simulation — jacking up default and correlation — shows the structure is robust by itself. The math is clean.

Yet investors price deals off the originator’s balance sheet, not the pool. Stronger originator, tighter spread; weaker originator, wider spread — almost regardless of what the underlying loans look like.

An SPV is created and the assets are transferred into a trust — but is the structure really bankruptcy-remote? As long as the originator is still collecting, it is not remote from the originator’s operations. Legally the pool is ring-fenced; operationally it isn’t. Until that operational gap is closed, spreads will not move no matter how clean the pool looks.

Originators should originate. Servicers should service.

Manufacturing figured this out long ago: specialisation at each stage of the assembly line is what creates efficiency and scale. Lending needs to do the same — and a dedicated third-party servicer solves this cleanly.

What a servicer of scale unlocks

Bankruptcy remoteness becomes real. If the originator fails, collections continue. Investors know this upfront, and pricing finally decouples from the originator’s balance sheet.

Smaller originators get market access. Today a mid-sized NBFC without a strong balance-sheet rating is effectively shut out of asset-backed lending. A credible servicer changes that — investors are betting on the pool, not the originator’s survival.

Geographical depth becomes possible. A servicer aggregating pools across originators in Surat, Ludhiana, Coimbatore and Tiruppur is a fundamentally different risk proposition than any single lender — diversification no originator can manufacture alone. All of it creates more credit depth.

Why it hasn’t happened

Originators don’t want to let go. The monthly collection touchpoint is the most intimate part of the customer relationship — recurring, data-rich, full of early credit-stress signals. Handing it to a third party feels like giving away the crown jewels. Collections is also a grey area, historically a game of “management.”

Onboarding is genuinely hard. Data-protection rules, vendor arrangements, borrower consent across multiple originators, systems and process integration — there is no easy template yet.

And it needs patient capital. A servicer of scale has thin margins, high operational intensity and slow network effects. It is a boring, messy business with deep trenches in legal operations — the kind a VC finds hard to touch. Some software and AI, yes, but at heart an operational business. Once the benefits start accruing, the market will follow.

But here’s why someone should build it anyway

In lending, power sits in exactly two places: whoever controls the customer, and whoever controls the capital. Everything else is commodity, or eventually becomes commodity — credit processes, software, even AI.

A servicer aggregating collections across originators and geographies quietly accumulates the most valuable thing in credit: customer intimacy at scale. Repayment behaviour, cashflow patterns, and early stress signals across thousands of borrowers that no single originator can see. A bureau-like business could be an offshoot — but at heart it is a valuable rail that creates credit depth.
