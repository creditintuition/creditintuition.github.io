A look back at fintech MSME lending

8 mintheme 02

In the early years of fintech lending, MSME credit appeared to be the more natural product.

Small businesses had to fund inventory, receivables and operating expenses before collecting cash from customers. Credit appeared to bridge a genuine gap between production and the cash-conversion cycle.

Consumer fintech lending, by comparison, often appeared to create demand and push borrowers into unnecessary credit cycles.

The experience since 2018 has made the comparison less straightforward. MSME credit addressed a real need, but the product and distribution structure created risks that were not fully visible during the initial growth phase.

1. Most loans were not true working-capital facilities

A working-capital facility should broadly match the borrower’s cash-conversion cycle. Exposure rises when inventory or receivables increase and reduces when the business collects cash.

Most fintech MSME loans were instead structured as amortising term loans, generally with tenors of two to three years.

A temporary funding requirement was therefore converted into a fixed monthly repayment obligation. The loan remained outstanding well beyond the inventory or receivable cycle for which it may originally have been taken.

The borrower could then take another loan for the next business cycle while the previous loan was still being repaid. Multiple loans described as working-capital finance could gradually become permanent leverage on the business.

A loan used for working capital was not necessarily structured as a working-capital loan.

2. The effective market was smaller and more concentrated than the headline MSME count

India’s large MSME base suggested a very large addressable market. In practice, fintech lenders could reach only a limited subset of businesses.

The borrower had to be sufficiently formal, banked, bureau-visible and operationally accessible. This concentrated lending among a smaller group of businesses, particularly in larger urban and commercial centres.

Physical collections were also a constraint. Origination could be digital, but difficult delinquency cases often required local verification, field visits and physical follow-up. Geographic expansion therefore required collection infrastructure, not only digital acquisition.

Several lenders and DSAs consequently competed for the same visible and reachable borrowers. This increased the possibility of multiple borrowing and overleveraging within a relatively narrow customer pool.

Digital origination expanded the funnel, but did not remove the physical infrastructure required to manage credit risk.

3. The ticket size was neither small nor large

The typical fintech MSME loan of a few lakh rupees occupied an operationally difficult segment.

The ticket was too large for a pure microcredit model based on small initial exposure, frequent repayments, telecollections and repeated short lending cycles.

At the same time, it was too small to support full SME underwriting involving detailed financial analysis, repeated site visits, supplier and customer checks, inventory assessment and continuous relationship management.

Lenders therefore relied on bureau rules, bank-statement analysis, GST data, scorecards and policy filters. These approaches required significant generalisation, while the individual exposure was large enough for underwriting errors to be costly.

The product was too large for a purely retail operating model, but too small to economically support detailed relationship-based SME underwriting.

4. Exposure was taken upfront, while learning came later

In a two- or three-year term loan, the full exposure was committed at the time of disbursement.

Meaningful repayment information became available only over the following months. By the time the performance of a customer segment, underwriting rule or sourcing channel became clear, several vintages of similar loans could already have been originated.

Short-cycle lending had a different risk structure. A lender could begin with a small amount, observe repayment behaviour and increase exposure gradually. Weak borrowers could be stopped after one or two cycles.

This did not make small-ticket lending inherently safer. It allowed exposure to be staged as information improved.

In the MSME term-loan model, exposure was front-loaded and learning was back-loaded.

Long tenors also supported rapid balance-sheet growth. Each loan remained outstanding for several years, while newer vintages had not yet seasoned. Portfolio growth could therefore remain strong before the full performance of earlier underwriting decisions became visible.

5. Limited direct reach to the customer

Many MSME fintech lenders scaled through DSAs and other sourcing partners rather than through direct customer acquisition.

The intermediary, rather than the lender, controlled a significant part of the customer flow. The lender could define its credit policy, but its ability to implement that policy independently was constrained by the commercial requirements of the sourcing relationship.

Lower approval rates, smaller ticket sizes, higher pricing or slower turnaround could reduce volumes or lead the DSA to shift business to another lender. Credit parameters therefore could not always be changed solely on the basis of portfolio performance.

This constraint was particularly important during the initial scaling phase, when the lender depended on the DSA for growth. The sourcing partner had influence over approval rates, ticket sizes, turnaround time and pricing, while the lender continued to carry the credit risk.

Long tenors made this more difficult. By the time weak performance from a sourcing channel became visible, multiple vintages could already be on the balance sheet. The lender could tighten new originations, but existing two- or three-year exposures remained.

Consumer fintech lenders developed stronger direct digital channels. This gave them greater control over customer acquisition, pricing, credit experimentation and origination volumes. They could scale up or scale back without being equally dependent on a small number of sourcing partners.

The difference was the degree of control over customer flow. In consumer lending, the lender increasingly owned the channel. In MSME lending, the channel often remained outside the lender.

Looking back

The original comparison between MSME and consumer fintech lending focused heavily on the purpose of the loan.

MSME credit financed a productive activity and addressed a visible funding gap. Consumer credit often appeared to finance discretionary consumption.

However, the products developed differently.

MSME fintech lending frequently combined term-loan structures, relatively large upfront exposure, long tenors, limited direct customer reach and dependence on intermediaries.

Consumer lenders, despite concerns around demand creation and borrower behaviour, developed direct digital channels and shorter feedback cycles. This gave them greater control over customer acquisition, pricing, experimentation and the pace of credit expansion.

This is only a look back at how the two models evolved after 2018. Product structures, distribution channels and underwriting capabilities continue to change. The comparison may look different again over the next two or three years.
