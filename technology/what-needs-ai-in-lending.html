What needs AI in a lending system — and what doesn’t

8 mindrawn from a live agentic build

The reflex, right now, is to put a model at every step. But look closely at a lending pipeline and most of it is not a judgment problem at all — it is a lookup, a rule, or a calculation. Those should stay deterministic. AI earns its place in a narrow set of tasks, and using it anywhere else quietly adds cost, latency, and a hallucination surface for no gain.

The cleanest way to design the stack is to sort every stage into one of three kinds of work.

AI agent — judgment / synthesis / reading messy inputsScript — rules, lookups, deterministic toolsHuman — the irreversible decision gate

Where AI genuinely earns its place

Three situations, and only three, actually need a model:

  • Reading messy, human-shaped inputs. Documents, bank statements, a recorded verification call, a field-investigation photo. The input has no fixed schema, so a deterministic parser breaks on the first unusual layout. OCR and vision-LLM extraction, call transcription, photo-authenticity checks — this is where models are load-bearing.
  • Synthesising over non-definitive factors. Preparing a credit memo, or a senior “reflection” that reads across the score, the call, and the first analyst’s note and forms a consolidated view. There is no rule that maps these inputs to an answer; the factors are open-ended and weigh against each other. That is judgment, and it is the real work of a credit agent.
  • Open-ended retrieval where the knowledge is genuinely non-definitive. A risk-factor knowledge base — where the factors are indicative, not rules — is the one honest use of RAG. If the answer is actually a fixed list, it is a table, not a retrieval problem.

Where deterministic script wins — and AI is a mistake

Everything with a knowable-in-advance answer should be compiled into a rule or a table. It is auditable, cheap, instant, never hallucinates, and a regulator can read it. A model here adds risk for zero benefit.

  • Document checklists. “What does this entity type need to submit?” is a lookup against a document-universe table, not a retrieval query.
  • Triangulation rules. Cross-checking extracted fields against each other is a compiled ruleset with a defined output tier, not a judgment call.
  • Policy, scoring, and limits. Loan-type and entity-type policy tables, the rule engine, bureau / bank-statement / GST API calls, the scoring and line-assignment APIs — all deterministic.
  • Plumbing. NACH verification, affidavit attachment, a 10% random-sample selector for review. Pure mechanics.

The trap worth naming: work that looks like knowledge is often just a lookup. In an early cut of the system, the checklist and the triangulation rules were built as RAG. They were then corrected to deterministic tables — same answer, but now testable and cheap. If the answer is knowable in advance, it is a table, not a model.

Where a human stays in the loop

Two kinds of steps should never be fully automated, no matter how good the model gets:

  • The irreversible credit decision. Reject, or proceed. A human owns it — for accountability and because the regulatory expectation is a human decision-maker on credit.
  • Genuine exceptions. A medium-risk escalation, a policy-cap breach (say a kuchcha-house limit). These are exactly the cases the rules were not built to resolve, so they route to a person.

The rule of thumb

  • Use AI only to read messy inputs, synthesise non-definitive judgment, or retrieve genuinely open-ended knowledge.
  • If the answer is knowable in advance, it is a table or a rule — not a model.
  • Every irreversible credit decision keeps a human gate.
  • An orchestration layer routes cases between these; it holds state, it does not judge.

Why the discipline matters

A model dropped onto a deterministic step doesn’t just cost more per call. It makes the step non-reproducible, harder to audit, and slower — and it introduces a small, permanent chance of a confidently wrong answer on work that had a correct answer all along. The credibility of an AI lending stack comes from being able to say exactly where a model is trusted and where it is not.

Put the model where judgment lives, keep everything knowable as script, and keep a human on every decision that cannot be taken back. The result is not less AI — it is AI that a risk committee, and a regulator, can actually stand behind.
