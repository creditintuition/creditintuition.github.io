MSME credit agent — system design

Most of an MSME lending pipeline is already deterministic. One stage is not: manual underwriting, where the judgment rests on common knowledge that cannot be codified and lives — undocumented and unaudited — inside individual underwriters. This is the design for an agent that captures that stage, keeping every irreversible call with a human.

The shape of the system

The common-knowledge track runs deterministic triggers alongside RAG retrieval over a precedent knowledge base. Its output feeds the persona panel — a set of idiosyncratic agents, each built from one underwriter’s LOS / case comments and case decisions — before an LLM synthesises the memo for the senior underwriter. The panel is an optional extension; the track and synthesis run with or without it.

What this deck covers

The problem

The one human bottleneck

Manual underwriting doesn’t scale with headcount, is not centrally codified, and leaves no audit trail for the qualitative reasoning applied.

The build

Deterministic where possible

Anything checkable runs as code, not retrieval. LLMs read messy inputs and synthesise judgment. RAG is used only for genuinely gestalt calls.

The unique piece

The persona panel

Optional agents, each modelled on a specific real underwriter with a proven track record, voting independently — with the vote spread preserved, not collapsed.

The persona panel — and where its knowledge comes from

Each persona is not a generic model. It is trained on the historical case decisions of a named senior underwriter whose outcomes are genuinely strong after controlling for the mix of cases they were assigned. Two gates apply before any persona is trusted: the underwriter’s track record must be real, and the AI must provably replicate their decisions on held-out cases — not just their writing style. See page 5.

Why this stage is the bottleneck

Manual underwriting through credit-memo drafting depends entirely on individual judgment. It is common knowledge that cannot be codified — not written into any central rulebook, not scalable with headcount, and carrying no systematic audit trail for the qualitative reasoning applied. It is the stage where organisational knowledge lives and dies with individual underwriters.

The senior underwriter’s final decision (stage 11) is retained unchanged — the agent replaces the drafting and reasoning that feeds it, not the authority.

What is kept

The human decision authority

The senior underwriter remains the final decision-maker in both systems. No irreversible credit call is automated.

The change-management point

A different review skillset

The senior UW shifts from checking a colleague’s reasoning to checking an AI’s evidence citations and reasoning trail. That is a training and rollout issue, not just a technical one — and should be called out explicitly.

03 · credit agent architecture

Inside the agent — deterministic first, model where judgment lives

The flow runs on a verified, classified application. Anything checkable is computed as code, because a silent recall failure in a retrieval system is worse than an explicit rule miss. RAG is reserved for genuinely gestalt calls.

3.1

Document extraction & triangulation

LLM+ rules

Vision + multilingual OCR extracts a fixed field list (standard OCR breaks across Indian regional languages). Extracted fields then run a rule-based triangulation engine — not an LLM, not RAG — cross-referencing across documents and government databases to produce contradiction flags.

↓
3.2

Business-type classification

inference layer

An upstream step several checks depend on. Combines the GST category as a weak prior, LLM inference from corroborating signals, and retrieval against a curated local precedent set. Must output a confidence / ambiguity indicator — a wrong label here silently corrupts every downstream check.

3.3 · common knowledge track — two parallel components

Trigger evaluation

deterministic

A rules table covering the atomic, relational, soft-cutoff and reference-list triggers. Computed, not retrieved — anything checkable is executed as code so a failure is never silent.

Precedent retrieval

RAG

Case-based retrieval limited to genuinely gestalt triggers (scale-vs-turnover plausibility, seasonality) where no deterministic rule exists and judgment is precedent-based.

↓
3.4

Idiosyncratic persona panel

optional

Multiple agents, each modelled on a specific strong underwriter, vote independently with the spread preserved. Gated on two validation tests before going live — detailed on the next page.

↓
3.5

Memo synthesis

LLM

Combines fired triggers with evidence, retrieved precedents, persona output (if enabled) and the documents into a draft credit memo plus a TVR question list — a standard set for all applicants, and a risk-clarification set generated from this case’s unresolved triggers.

↓
3.6

TVR loop

case-scoped

Customer answers feed a rerun of memo synthesis for that same application, producing an updated memo. Ephemeral per case — it never touches the knowledge base.

↓
3.7

Senior UW decision

human gate

Reviews the completed memo (post-TVR, with panel dissent if enabled) and makes the final call — same authority as today. Two feedback cycles then update the knowledge base:

Weekly

Candidate rule proposals from patterns in UW corrections and agreement rates. Proposals only — changes require human review before shipping.

Quarterly

Outcome-validated recalibration against realised early-default (DPD) performance. The slower, higher-confidence gate — and where personas are re-validated.

3.4 · the unique extension

The idiosyncratic persona panel

The distinctive piece of the design. Rather than one averaged model, a panel of agents — each a faithful replica of a specific, named underwriter — votes independently, and the disagreement between them is treated as signal, not noise.

Where the knowledge comes from

Each persona is built from the historical case decisions of a real senior underwriter whose track record is genuinely strong after controlling for case-mix. The source is that person’s own decision history — the very tribal knowledge that otherwise stays locked inside them — turned into an agent that can be interrogated, audited and re-validated.

The spread is preserved, never collapsed

A 5–0 and a 3–2 outcome carry different information for the senior UW even when the headline recommendation is identical. Aggregation keeps the distribution and shows it as a visual gauge alongside the memo.

5 – 0
Unanimous approve — high confidence, little to probe.
3 – 2
Same headline “approve”, but genuine dissent — a flag for the senior UW to look closer.

Two validation gates — before any persona is trusted

1
Track-record validity
The underlying underwriter’s performance is genuinely good — controlling for the kind of cases they were assigned, not just raw portfolio outcome. Case allocation must be reasonably uniform across the underwriters being compared for the comparison to be valid.
2
Persona fidelity
The AI persona actually replicates that underwriter’s judgment — not just their comment-writing style. Tested by predicting the underwriter’s historical decisions on held-out cases before the persona’s vote is trusted.

Personas require periodic re-validation — proposed on the quarterly outcome-recalibration cycle — since case-mix and underwriter performance drift over time. The panel is a later extension, gated on both tests, not part of the initial build.

Some triggers must never fire on their own

Proxy ownership and applicant substitution demand a high evidentiary bar and human sign-off — they must never fire on family structure alone, and carry a real risk of penalising genuine family-run businesses if applied mechanically. PEP screening is pulled out entirely as a deterministic regulatory gate under RBI KYC directions, never left inside the fuzzy layer.
